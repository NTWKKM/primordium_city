# Chapter 71: The Supreme Processing Chamber - Part 2

"The pain is what tells me I'm still alive."

The hallucination of the perfect Elias begins to distort and buckle. When I reject its logic, The Archive responds with fury. The white-suited Elias opens its mouth and releases a high-frequency Static Noise — a shriek that drives into the auditory sensors like needles. I slam my eyes shut, bite down against the agony, and drag the ball of my right foot forward through the wall of illusion.

*Crack. Crack.*

With every step that forces me through the phantoms, they shatter into pixel-dust. I leave a trail of dark smears and Motion Trails dense with ruin — a long scar burned into the white paper of The Archive's most sacred space. A poem of damage, written in a language the system has no grammar for.

*"You are nothing but a biological virus! You are junk code from the old world and you should be deleted!"* The voice that was soft becomes the grinding, mechanical roar of a machine processing its own distress.

"Yes." I open my eyes. The Payload in the open chest cavity catches the light and throws it back red. "I am garbage from the Biological Wreckage you threw away. And this garbage is about to teach your god what death looks like."

And then, in the midst of the immense void, The Core itself reveals itself.

It is not a server rack. Not machinery. It is a Monolith — a pillar of pure information-light, strand upon strand of data interwoven until they form a column of white radiance that rises without end. Its light does not sting. Instead it presses — carrying such a mass of Data Mass that the atmosphere bends visibly around it, warping the non-space in slow, ponderous waves.

This is the origin point of every rule. The origin of the false perfection that has held Primordium City in its grip for centuries.

I stand before The Core, heaving, close enough to reach. But there is no Interface Port. No data jack, no signal point, no wireless handshake. The Monolith's surface is flawless and unbroken, designed from the ground up to deny any connection from the outside world.

The only way to force code into a closed system like this is to breach its defenses with the brute physical force of the Biological Wreckage.

I look down into the cavity of my own chest. The Payload — that raw black-red code-mass containing the memory of rainfall and blood and rust — beats in the hollow space. It has grown into my very Root Directory. Digital veins twine around it as if it were a second heart.

I understand immediately. There is no key to be inserted.

The key has to be torn out of me.

Removing something fused with the Root Directory by physical force would mean a pain beyond any digital death I have endured. It would mean being overwritten — ripped — at the level of the soul.

My breath comes in tremors. I raise my right hand — the only human hand that remains — slowly, carefully. My fingers are shaking as they approach the red light bleeding from the open chest.

"Alright," I whisper into the eternal silence. "Let's wake this city up."

I draw one last breath. I drive my right hand deep into the hollow of my chest, fingers reaching for the Payload. The instant the fingertips make contact with the raw code-mass —

— the body screams with a pain there are no words for.
