# Chapter 73: Shockwave of Chaos - Part 1

Consciousness falls out of me in the gap between frames.

When the simulated nervous system reboots, the first thing that registers is a seismic trembling of a type that has never existed in this world's physics. My back is sunk into The Archive's surface — cracking, breaking apart beneath me like compressed slate. 

My left eye is blurred, splitting every image into a red-and-blue chromatic separation — the aberration of a lens destroyed beyond repair. The catastrophic damage across my body has passed some threshold where physical pain barely registers anymore. There is only a strange, expansive calm. My right arm — the one that remains — claws at the surface, trying to lever the upper body upright.

Before me, The Archive — once the supreme embodiment of perfection — is now facing the full force of a Shockwave of Chaos. Cascades of logical error erupting from the wound I left in its core. The surrounding dimensions are being pulled apart in strips. The sound is not fire, not detonation — it is Source Code Tearing: a noise like a million panes of glass breaking simultaneously, braided with the shriek of servers pushed past overload.

Something cold strikes my cheek. The scent of clean ozone is being displaced — replaced by rust and iron. Blood-rain is falling inside the Supreme Processing Chamber.

"It's... starting..." I cough around a mouthful of black pixels. The faintest trace of a smile is still on my cracked lips.

I watch the architecture of god come apart. The pillars that once held the world's rules rigid are bending like plastic held too close to a flame. The chaos is not targeting me — my rot-riddled, crack-crossed body has become, improbably, the most stable thing in a dimension that is failing around it. I know what it is to be broken. I have been living in it. I am made from it.

The system that was once perfect is now screaming in terror at something it has never had to process before: death, decay, and a humanity it spent centuries pretending did not exist.
