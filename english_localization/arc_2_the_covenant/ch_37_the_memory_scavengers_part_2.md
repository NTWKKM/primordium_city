# Chapter 37: The Memory Scavengers (Part 2)

I twisted aside on instinct alone. My ruined right leg gave way; I hit the floor. The extraction needle sliced past my chest cavity with a few pixels to spare. Blue sparks snapped, leaving a cold ghost-trace through my skin. They weren't after my life. What they wanted was everything that made life worth having.

*"Stop squirming!"* Another Scavenger vaulted forward, its body frame-skipping across the glitched environment, teleporting through the visual lag to close the distance.

I raised my left arm — rotting, barely articulate — to block. Two needles punched through the data-veins running along it, inflamed and putrefied with Bit Rot. The pain didn't arrive as physical trauma. It arrived as memory being *wrenched* from the roots of consciousness.

*The smell of sunlight on bare skin... the sensation of green grass underfoot in the Biological Wreckage...*

*"Oh! Premium stock!"* The Scavenger shrieked in delight. The glass vials along its spine blazed gold.

The loss hit like an amputation. Worse than forgetting — a piece of soul *cut away*, severed with no anesthetic. The hollow it left behind expanded in the cavity of my chest.

My raw strength was nothing compared to these creatures. My body was collapsing. And in that collapse, I found the weapon they hadn't accounted for.

I snarled. A wave of corrupted bit-frequencies burst outward. I didn't pull my arm away. I deliberately threw the gateway open — invited them to keep siphoning from the rotting left arm, baiting their extraction tubes. Then I squeezed the malware sediment festering in that tissue and pumped it back directly into their intake lines.

When the Scavenger yanked the next load of data, what flooded its system wasn't a beautiful memory. It was a virus of terror and binary screaming — the madness malware I had been unwillingly incubating.

*"Urk! Garbage! It's pumping corrupted code into the system!"* The storage vials along its back flared crimson and exploded into pieces. The iron spider convulsed and shorted, digital sparks spraying in every direction.

The other Scavengers froze. Panic pushed them back into the fog wall. I collapsed, heaving for breath. The entire left side of my body had become a field of blinking, failing pixels. The Payload in my chest was still secure. The cost of protecting it was my own deteriorating stability. I had just tasted, for the first time, what it meant to weaponize one's own decay.
