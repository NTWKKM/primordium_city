# Chapter 22: The Compressed Memory Bazaar - Part B

I limped through the crushing crowd. Every time our shoulders collided, I felt static sparks flash, accompanied by a deep ache from my decaying left arm. It was a hitbox collision calculated incorrectly by the system. The scene around me was pure chaos—buyers and sellers bargaining frantically.

At a stall across the way, a young woman was sobbing. Instead of natural tears, blue pixels dripped from the corners of her eyes, shattering into raw code the moment they hit the ground.

“Please...” her voice cracked like glass. “Let me buy back the *smell of rain on dry soil*. I just sold it to you yesterday. Now... I can't remember what rain is. I'm losing it!”

The merchant shook his head callously. “Sold already, little girl. Someone traded three days of processing quota for that rain smell. Find something else... I have the *grief of breaking a glass cup*. Cheap. It’s quite effective at stimulating simulated neural pathways.”

The girl collapsed to the ground, shrieking in a high-pitched, ear-splitting frequency—binary screams. Her body spasmed violently as her skin began to lose its texture, turning into a flat, matte-grey mannequin. She couldn't process the reality, and her processing system was collapsing right before our eyes.

No one stopped to look at her. The others kept their heads down, selecting fragments of emotion from the stalls.

I stared at the scene, a heavy weight sinking into the deepest part of my chest. Fear crept in to consume my mind—not the fear of death, but the dread of *forgetting*.

Did I still remember the feeling of the breeze through the windows of the Inner Ring? Did I still recall the softness of clean bedsheets? Or had those high-resolution details already been compressed and slowly erased from my personal database?

If I stayed here too long, I too would have to carve out pieces of my love, my rage, or my hope to hawk on the street, exchanging them for someone else’s fake sensations just to prove to a simple logic loop that I was still 'alive'.

The economy of the Mid-Sectors was not driven by wealth or power. It was driven by yearning.

A yearning for a humanity we might never have possessed in the first place.

My breath puffed out another cloud of grey mist. I shoved my left arm, bloated with purple compression artifacts, into my coat pocket. My right hand remained guardedly pressed against the Data Key in my chest. I knew the Hounds of the system were tracking my high-UX traces, closing in. Turning my back on that tragic bazaar, I forced my dissolving frame to walk forward into the blur of the mirrored city.
