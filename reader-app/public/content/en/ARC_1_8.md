# Logs of Lost Resolution

I was back in 'perfection' once more.

Golden-hour radiance spilled through the simulated window, bathing the alabaster white walls of my quarters. The environment of the Inner Ring was flawless—devoid of dust, scratches, or any physical friction. It was the 32K-resolution sanctuary I inhabited.

In a world where even the smell of sweat was banned, the scent of rotting meat drifted into my nostrils. A metallic tang of copper and thick, coppery blood pooled at the base of my tongue. These were phantom biological sensations—the first warning signs of Stage 1 Bit Rot.

I had barely survived smuggling the Zero-Byte payload—the biological wreckage I had recovered—past the security scanners of the Inquisition. Stashing it deep within my avatar's rendering pipeline was a suicidal gamble. As I crossed the scanning arches, I had to overclock my processing cores to their absolute limit, forcing the system to render my outer shell in flawless 32K to mask the fractures of the biological code embedded within. Drawing such massive resources saved me from detection, but it came at the cost of an overheating warning that still seared my processing core, like a lump of molten iron lodged in my chest all the way back to my quarters.

Cold sweat beaded along my hairline—even though my avatar was never programmed to sweat.

I stood before the digital mirror in my room, trying to pull myself together. An invisible weight pressed down on my shoulders, as if an immense gravity were dragging my structure down. I had to manually override my rigging system, locking my joints and forcing my posture upright to keep my shoulders from collapsing under the weight.

When I turned my head, my reflection lagged by a fraction of a second. My eyes in the mirror stared back at me with a skin-crawling latency. It was a somatic breach, a sign that deep within this digital body... something was slowly rotting away.

My time in these quarters was short. The chime of my daily schedule rang out. I had no choice but to carry this decay inside me and head to the Synthesis Bureau—a place crawling with the eyes of The Archive. I had to go there to find a way to decode the payload before its heat burned me to cinders.

The offices of the Synthesis Bureau lulled everyone with a low hum designed to project safety and serene calm.

But for me, beneath that relaxing low frequency, I could feel a searing heat building under my skin. The hiss of static bled into my neural pathways, like a slowly short-circuiting wire echoing in my ears alone.

Valeria stood in the central corridor. She was still clad in her shimmering, premium attire, but her elegant posture seemed fragile. She stood with arms crossed unusually tight, her fingertips digging into the simulated fabric. The arrogance that once defined her eyes had been replaced by an indelible paranoia ever since the cafe incident. She was straining to maintain her mask of perfection, the terror hiding behind it so palpable I could almost smell it.

The moment I drew near, her gaze snapped to me.

"You look... unwell, Elias," she greeted me. Her voice was forced low, striving for flatness, yet there was a tremor at the edges like a poorly encoded audio file. "You haven't... run into anything that would cause your resolution quota to drop, have you?"

I knew she was using me as a mirror to verify her own status. She wanted to see if the decay had spread to me yet. At the same time, the payload in my rendering pipeline was beginning to take its toll. A micro-stutter was clawing its way to the surface.

I had learned from my previous lesson that feigning weakness or staging 'Curated Chaos' would never deceive The Archive. So, I did not suppress it. I let the genuine biological pain of carrying this payload spill over.

In a fraction of a second, the rigging lock I had forced to support my left shoulder failed, shattering completely. My body convulsed in an uncontrollable spasm. My left shoulder slumped under the simulated gravity, and the digital coffee in my hand splattered across my sleeve. A sharp pain shot from my chest to my brain, forcing a low groan from my throat. It was a real, physical collapse erupting beneath the skin.

I had to force a smile, marshalling every ounce of concentration to lock my balance system and force my avatar to stand upright amidst the rapid glitches flickering at the edges of my vision.

Valeria recoiled half a step, her eyes widening with a renewed panic. She hastily recomposed her expression and turned away. "The... the system demands perfection, Elias. Don't go acting like a walking corpse around here."

She hurried off in a frenzy, desperate to distance herself from her greatest fear, completely unaware that the corpse she spoke of was not merely walking—it had already infiltrated the core of the system.
