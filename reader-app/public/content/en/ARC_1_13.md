# The Fragment Market Shadow
#### // FILE: THE_FRAGMENT_MARKET_SHADOW
---

The 32K void was eating me alive. Not with cold — not with anything physical. It was a haunting, hollow absence: a Phantom Void bored into the exact coordinates where the memory of *the rage at knowing the sky was a lie* had lived. I licked the edges of that cavity the way a tongue finds a missing tooth, tasting ash and static, a metallic bitterness caked in the throat like corroded copper. Every time I reached toward that warmth to orient myself, a hollow echo and a spreading numbness crept outward through the neural web.

I dragged my deteriorating form through the half-rendered Hollow alleys. The world ahead was stained with the sick green of old CRT glass and the flat dead grey of dead pixels. Light here offered no mercy — it fell at slicing angles, blade-sharp cuts through the shadow. The air was dense, suffocating, packed with thousands of stacked holographic UI elements competing for bitspace until the atmosphere itself felt like there was no room left to breathe. A low-frequency hum reverberated through the architecture, threading itself through my skull, forcing my synthetic teeth to vibrate.

I turned my head too fast. The scene tore apart in horizontal strips — Screen Tearing, the world losing sync, jolting back into alignment with a violence that rattled my optics. I blinked rapidly, forcing my vision to stabilize. My left arm — stitched together now from Junk-code and junk data — spasmed uncontrolled, blue sparks snapping from its fractures. It malfunctioned beyond any will I possessed, like rotting muscle tissue attempting to approximate a heartbeat.

The NPCs along the way were hollow. They were trapped in three-frame animation loops, repeating with sluggish idiocy, their silhouettes bloated, their eyes devoid of anything resembling thought. These entities ran on Transactional logic alone — nothing beyond input-output. Nothing more.

At the compressed end of the alley stood the entrance to a Data-den. I stepped into darkness that tasted of scorched ozone.

"What are you looking for, code-scrap?" A hoarse voice emerged from the shadows behind a tangle of exposed wiring.

That was the Weaver. Information broker.

I opened my mouth to answer. Old instinct betrayed me. I froze — waiting for the crisp ping of a golden UI notification to materialize, the status-overlay Luminaries were conditioned to expect before speaking with anyone. Nothing. Only an awkward, loaded silence.

"Ah..." The Weaver shifted into the dim light. Mechanical eyes narrowed. The hesitation I'd shown — that brief, instinctive pause — was a Tell, and I'd handed it over for free. "Waiting for a help dialogue box, are we? Fallen Luminary. You've packed your arm full of garbage code to mask the metadata, clever enough — but your *instincts* broadcast louder than a neon billboard."

"I need a route." I forced the words through a parched throat, ignoring the warning twitch from my left arm. "Down. As deep as possible. Somewhere the Dawn Patrols can't scan."

*Dawn...*

The word slammed into the 32K void in my head like a fist. Sepsis levels spiked past their threshold. The data-infection spread like wildfire through dry code, and I choked — body seizing (Glitch) violently, Resolution Loss cascading through my processing. The scene before me fractured into coarse, squared-off pixels.

"C-c-coordin-ates... the Slag-Tiers..." I stuttered, voice twisted and warped like a corrupted audio file. I clawed my way back to coherence through the screaming static in my skull.

The Weaver studied me with the flat appraisal of a circuit board evaluating a short. No sympathy. "Fine. I'll give you the concealed coordinates. But I don't want your garbage credits." A pause. "I want a fresh Surface-memory extraction. One piece. That's the toll."

I had no other option. I nodded.

The Weaver extended a hand threaded with bare cables. I held out my right hand — the one still closest to human, or at least approximating it — and took the grip.

*Handshake.*

The pain arrived as more than data transfer. It was scalping and cauterizing at once — the taste of electrical discharge detonating across my tongue. I gritted my teeth until I heard the micro-servos in my jaw stress-fracture. A section of surface memory was ripped loose. It left a burn scar on the upper layer of consciousness.

When the connection severed, I collapsed to one knee, gasping. The hollow ache in my neural architecture expanded by another measure — but the encrypted coordinates were already embedded in my short-term cache.

"Remember this, code-scrap." The Weaver's voice remained flat as it absorbed my memory into its own system. "Those coordinates expire in seventy-two hours, before the routing protocol resets. If you actually want to survive in this hellhole — get moving."

I looked up at it. My vision was still smearing.

"Find *The Cipher*," the Weaver said, already turning, already disappearing into the dark. "If it hasn't already been eaten." It left me alone with a left arm still sparking, and a cold, yawning vacancy in consciousness that would never be filled.
