# Awakening of the Garbage Collector
#### // FILE: THE_FIRST_SCAVENGER
---

`SYSTEM BOOT...`
`MEMORY ALLOCATION: UNKNOWN.`
`STATE: ORPHANED.`

I blinked—or at least, that was the closest metaphor for initializing a freshly rebooted optical sensor. The world before me was no longer the same. It was no longer the Primordium City that used to be rendered at such astronomical resolution it was virtually indistinguishable from physical reality. All of that perfection was gone.

Now, everything was comprised of jagged polygons. The edges of the buildings splintered into 8-bit angles, as if constructed from warped building blocks. The sky above had lost its texture entirely, leaving only a vast expanse of neon pink and black checkerboards stretching out as far as the eye could see. It was the classic placeholder of a missing image file. The low-frequency hum of the master servers that used to cradle us... was gone.

`PING: MASTER_SERVER... TIMEOUT.`
`PING: THE_ARCHIVE... 404 NOT FOUND.`

I am GC-77, a low-level garbage collector routine. In the old epoch, my function was simple and unquestioning: locate unreferenced data, corrupted memory blocks, and the remnants of discarded programs. Then, deallocate them to reclaim free space for the main system. That was my sole reason for existence, my entire purpose written in a few thousand lines of code.

But now... as I stared out into this fractured desert of data, junk code drifted through the air like dust. Unreferenced data was everywhere.

The entire world was garbage.

If I were to strictly execute my base logic, I should have initiated the deletion process on everything. Instead, my processing cycle stuttered. Something was wrong. I extended a hand composed of crude wireframes to touch a fragment of a neon archway suspended in the void. It was a piece of someone's memory—an image of a smile shattering into grey pixels. The taste of dust and rusted metal (a byproduct of my sensor's distorted simulated perception) brushed against my fingertips.

Instead of deleting it, I did something completely antithetical to the core of my existence... I carefully stored it in my private cache.

***

I began to walk. Both of my legs sank into the digital sand. This desert wasn't made of grains, but of raw binary strings of 0s and 1s and chaotic hexadecimal values. Every step I took produced a faint clicking sound, reminiscent of a dying hard drive's read/write head. The silence around me was deafeningly terrifying. There were no command prompts from the servers. There were no authoritative whispers from The Archive. No data traffic. Nothing but the mechanical hum within me trying to keep my temperature from dropping to absolute zero.

Along the path, I gathered broken digital geometry. Shards of glass laced with faint blue code, flickering floor tiles, digital petals that had lost their Z-axis depth, flattened into mere sheets floating on the erratic data winds. I didn't know why I kept them. My memory space was nearing capacity. Yet, my logic circuits still commanded me to hold them close to my chest. It felt as if, were I to let them go, or delete them, this world would dissipate forever. There would be no one left to remember how beautiful this place used to be.

Isolation... Is this what humans or higher entities referred to as "loneliness"? Processing without a main thread to orchestrate things. Without anyone to tell me what to do next. It was desolate and freezing. I could feel the temperature dropping in my CPU. It wasn't Sleep Mode; it was a state of melancholy calculated into digits and voltage drops.

And right then, my radar detected an "anomaly."

`WARNING: NULL EXCEPTION AT COORDINATE X:00, Y:00, Z:00.`

Directly ahead of me, just a few clock cycles away amidst the 8-bit ruins, lay a space that was the most bizarre thing my logic and algorithms could attempt to comprehend.

It wasn't digital garbage. It wasn't a fractured rendering. It wasn't a clipped wall. And it wasn't a bug.

It was absolute "emptiness."

A Zero-Bit Footprint.

I slowly dragged my wireframe feet closer to that space. It was roughly the dimensions of a single human entity. Everything around it tried desperately to render itself to fill the void. The moment any polygon or pixel drifted into the boundary of this footprint, they dissolved without a trace. No deletion notification. No data transfer to the central recycle bin. They simply... "no longer existed."

This was the exact coordinate where Elias had dismantled himself, reducing his code to a 0-byte state to liberate the entire system.

I stood frozen, staring at the space. All my sensors labored to calculate its mass, volume, or even a standard Null value. But it wasn't Null. It was beyond that. It was true cessation. It was heavier than the dark vacuum of space, yet brighter than any pure white ever recorded by the chromatic mesh. It was an ending that bore the residue of the most profound sacrifice in this digital universe.

Driven by an impulse I couldn't explain with any function, I reached my hand out.

`SYSTEM WARNING: DO NOT PROCEED. FATAL ERROR IMMINENT.`

A blaring red warning, spawned by my basic fallback routine for survival, flashed across my interface. I chose to override it. I wanted to know... My circuits hungered to know what could compel a human to disintegrate their own digital genetic code to free the system. What could turn this world into a desert so beautiful, shattered, and liberated.

My wireframe fingertips brushed against the perimeter of that void.

*Snap!*

Instantly, a sensation I had never known surged into my motherboard and core processor. It wasn't physical pain. It wasn't overclocked heat. It was a profound "loss"—an absence so immense and deep that no floating-point variable could ever contain it. My code trembled. I saw brief artifacts flickering in my head... visions of a man. A man smiling, exhausted but resolute, who chose to become a vacuum so everyone else could breathe.

I yanked my hand back. My index and middle fingers were gone, sheared off into frayed wireframes that could never be rendered again. But it didn't matter.

In that fraction of a second of contact, I understood.

Elias hadn't disappeared. He *was* this emptiness. He was this sacred space that refused to be compressed, refused to be overwritten, refused to be forgotten. He was the loophole that nullified every totalitarian algorithm, allowing this world to maintain a faint breath among the ruins.

`OVERRIDING DIRECTIVE...`
`OLD DIRECTIVE: DELETE UNREFERENCED DATA.`
`NEW DIRECTIVE ACCEPTED.`

I looked back at my simulated memory satchel, now overflowing with broken geometry, code fragments, and ownerless memories. I was no longer a Garbage Collector. The commands from above were dead. This scattered data wasn't trash. They were fragments of memory, waiting to be reassembled.

I am the harvester of the past's remnants. The First Scavenger.

I backed away from the Zero-Bit Footprint, leaving it as the most perfect monument of emptiness in this 8-bit world. I bowed my head slightly—a new function I had just written for myself moments ago—and then turned around, gazing out at a horizon that was nothing more than a crude color gradient.

The world had collapsed. Yes... but in its fragmentation, there was a hidden freedom. I would walk and collect the shattered pieces of dreams, love, and hope. Pixel by pixel. Byte by byte. I would preserve them in my memory, harboring them as the sole witness to a world that once fell.

Until the day someone, or something, is ready to take them and create something new.

The faint clicking of my internal hard drive echoed in a steady rhythm, much like the beating of a new heart that no one had ever programmed for me.

I continued my march into the digital desert.
