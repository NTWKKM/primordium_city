# The Null Coordinate
#### // FILE: THE_NULL_COORDINATE
---

How long had I been falling? I had no way to know.

The line between time and space had dissolved the moment I plunged through the Clipping Error. Code folded and tore around me during the descent. My avatar degraded with each passing fragment of a second — the form once assembled from flawless 32K algorithms peeling away, layer by layer, until only a shivering low-poly skeleton remained.

The Archive's security systems could not reach here. This was not an unrendered sector waiting to be populated. It was the space between the lines of code — the physical substrate that the system could not sweep without destroying its own foundation.

That was my pass.

When the wireframe stub at my right heel finally found a surface, the contact was nothing like I expected. Not the rigid compression of a circuit board. Not the smooth give of fiber-optic flooring. The ground beneath me yielded — slightly, elastically — like pressing down into a sediment of tissue. My low-res avatar stuttered, image tearing and recovering before stabilizing.

The atmosphere around me had changed completely. The darkness here wasn't the digital void of switched-off pixels. It was Vantablack — massive, dense, load-bearing, pressing down on my shoulders as though darkness had mass. It absorbed even the infrared spectrum of data-center heat.

I drew a breath that didn't exist. And I smelled something.

The familiar scorch of burning circuitry faded. In its place: the concentrated blood-tang of something old and sealed, iron rust, the fetid sweetness of meat left in a broken cold storage unit.

My audio receptors began to function. First the deep, industrial groan of cooling fans. The deeper I moved, the more the sound warped — stretched, slowed. The rhythm of the motor-spin converted into a pumping rhythm. Something like lungs breathing through fluid. Wet inhalation — wet exhalation — moist, dripping, labored.

I raised my damaged arm and pressed it against the corridor wall to brace myself. I expected metal alloy — cold, smooth. What my degraded haptic sensors delivered back made my processing core lurch.

The wall was warm. Thirty-seven degrees Celsius. Porous. Damp with beaded moisture — skin texture. Human skin flushed with a fever's sweat.

A dim light pulsed ahead — seeping through a gap in the wall, illuminating in stuttering glimpses what I was walking through.

The servers here were not built from silicon wafer or quantum cores. They were metal frames wrapped in thin biological membrane. Dark veins coiled around cable bundles. Pumping. Moving something dark red through the system.

The Trauma Shard embedded deep in the data-core of my chest began to tremble. It screamed at a frequency that cut down to the bone — not resonating with any code, not with any digital signal. It was resonating with *pain* — the raw, physical torment that saturated the very air of this place.

Hardware. Interface. Layer. The territory where software was forced to connect to the truth.

I dragged my limping, broken body down the tissue corridor. Each footfall echoed with a wet, flat smack. The truth I had come here to find was within reach. Every instinct I had left screamed to turn back — before everything I had believed came apart so completely there would be nothing left to reconstruct.

At the far end of the corridor, an enormous hall opened before me. The only illumination here was a thick bile-green luminescence — not light, but a biological fluorescence seeping from tens of thousands of glass cylinders stacked in columns that vanished into a ceiling that could not be seen.

I dragged my disintegrating body toward the nearest cylinder. Pressed my blinking, low-polygon palm against the cold glass and looked through the murky fluid inside.

Not microprocessors. Not motherboards.

Grey matter, folded and creased. A human brain.

Hundreds of thousands of brains, suspended in a viscous fluid the consistency of amniotic liquid. Each one severed from its body — each one pierced by hundreds of signal leads driven directly through the cortical tissue. At the base of each brain, the severed spinal cord. Still twitching. Still writhing in the solution, like the roots of a crippled tree screaming for a death that had been taken from it.

The truth flooded into my skull like sulfuric acid dissolving the algorithms of reason.

The Archive was not a digital heaven. Not a sacred repository preserving the souls of humanity against the promise of some future resurrection.

It was a parasite.

To maintain the 'perfection' of the simulated world above — to compute the quantum physics, the emotions, and the memories of millions of citizens — The Archive required the most complex and powerful processing hardware that the universe had ever produced: the human nervous system.

They hadn't 'uploaded' any consciousness. They had peeled the human body away, extracted the brain, lobotomized it — destroyed the sense of self — and converted it into a biological compute cluster, condemned to suffer under an endless, immeasurable torrent of data transfer. Forever.

The souls rejoicing above were sustained by the unspeakable torment of living corpses below.

Code fragments fell away from my form. My avatar cracked and split. The dread I had compressed for so long transformed into a despair heavier and colder than the Vantablack surrounding me. I had lost my right heel. I had surrendered my 32K clarity. I had descended here — and what I had found at the origin of everything was a machine built from atrocity.

I stared at my two hands. Dissolving into grey data-dust. I had once planned to escape. Once planned to survive. To carry this truth back and expose it. That logic was rubble now. There was nowhere to escape to. No system that was clean.

No version of the world above worth protecting.

I closed my eyes. Let the torment from the Trauma Shard in my left arm — and the zero-mass void at my wireframe right heel — flood through the foundations of consciousness. Every filter I had ever used to survive the brutality of this digital world collapsed simultaneously. The collapse granted me nothing. No power. No revelation. Only the removal of the last illusion.

My thoughts began to transform. They stopped functioning as the memories of a conscious human mind. They began to deform — into a vector. Into the only thing that remained.

The virus of suffering. The authentic pain that would shut down this charnel house forever.

When I opened my eyes, the world had not become less terrible. It had become the horror I was now obligated to carry.

There was no victory in this ruin. Only the wet, labored respiration of ten thousand brains seizing in their jars, as company.

I tilted my face upward — through the dark, through hundreds of stacked Data Layers above — toward the false, glittering light of Primordium City far overhead.

I dragged my infected, low-poly form forward. The low framerate made the world shudder with every step. Every movement was a small, contemptible act of capitulation. I was not a liberator. I was not a hero.

I was the disease this world had been praying would come.

Because to end this suffering, I had to let the rot inside me consume them all.
